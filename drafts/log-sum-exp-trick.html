Underflow and the log-sum-exp trick

Some time, you may need to compute the logarithm of a sum of very small numbers, e.g., when computing quantities for reasonably long sequences under a HMM model.

To be concrete, consider \(S = \log\sum_{i=1}^n e^{a_i}\) when \(a_i \ll 0\) and thus \(e^{a_i} > 0\) are super small. Computing \(S\) in this case may result in underflow. To avoid the issue, we can use the log-sum-exp trick. Let \(b = \max_{i=1}^n a_i\), then \(S = \log e^b\sum_{i=1}^n e^{a_i-b} = b + \log\sum_{i=1}^n e^{a_i-b}\). Why is this better? At least, \(a_i-b \geq a_i\), and we extract the significant value \(b\) out of the sum. Despite the name, the key idea here is the shift by subtracting the maximum value, which makes the numerical computation more stable.

Intuitively, \(S \approx max_{i=1}^n a_i\).
